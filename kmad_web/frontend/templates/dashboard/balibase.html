BAliBASE

This page might create the incorrect impression that BAliBASE is larded with errors. It is not. We merely want to make clear that a small percentage of the BAliBASE gold-standard alignments are open for discussion, and might have multiple correct solutions, depending on the point of view of the scientist, or on the question asked.

KMAD was originally designed for the alignment of intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs). Since we use motif-, domain-, and post-translational modification- (PTM-) information to optimize the alignments we were a bit puzzled how to make the referees happy who requested that we design a validation method to objectively establish the quality of KMAD's alignments. We performed validation on two alignment benchmarks, i.e. PREFAB and BAliBASE. We used both the structure-based BAliBASE 3.0 and the BAliBASE motif reference set. How we managed to get rid of the circularity of using motifs to improve the alignment and using the alignment of the motifs to see how well the alignment went, is described in the article.

This section deals with some of the problems associated with motif alignment.

Correct motif alignment is an extremely difficult task to perform, even by manual curation. There are situations, where the 'correct' solution is not obvious or it does not even exist at all. Therefore, it comes as no surprise that there are alignments in BAliBASE for which ultimate solutions seem equally correct. We provide here several examples (along with two examples from BAliBASE for structured proteins) to illustrate the intrinsic ambiguity of IDP sequence alignment.

In two cases we did not understand why BAliBASE alignments were as they were. Fortunately these two cases were explained to us by one of the BAliBASE creators, prof. Julie Thompson. (We include her answers in the text).

We found many examples where the BAliBASE creators overruled the ELM motif assignment. Even though KMAD often agrees with BAliBASE curators one should actually manually validate all cases where it does not. In summary, we believe that problems associated with annotation and curation are responsible for a few percent of the total scores. Given that the BAliBASE scores for the methods tested (T-Coffee, MAFFT, Clustal Omega, ClustalW2, and MUSCLE) differ by at most a few percent too we have to conclude that all methods tested perform equally well considering this margin of error.

Unless stated otherwise, the alignments listed below, are BAliBASE reference alignments. The bright orange residues are residues included in the TP/FN motif instances by BAliBASE, and purple background residues are the segments that match the ELM pattern.

How is it determined which motifs are true positives?
Alignment BOX230 from the reference 9, subset RV921
Motif: LIG_FHA_2
Regex: "..(T)..[DE]."

This is one of the examples where we asked BAliBASE to explain us the reason of their choices.

1. The LIG_FHA_2 motif is annotated (by a human) in the top (first) sequence, but it is only predicted in the other sequences. When there are multiple occurrences of the motif in other sequences how is it decided which of them should be aligned with the one in the top sequence?

2. Why is the motif in the 5th sequence shifted to the left? If we assume that all of them are true positives, shouldn't they all be lined up?

3. Concerning the false negative motif in the 9th sequence. Why do you assume that the prediction (or actually lack of prediction) is wrong and it's not just a case of a motif changing it's position? Motifs very often 'move around' in the disordered regions.

Answers:

1. We chose the motif that is aligned with the annotated sequence based on the sequence conservation score of the motifs and their relative positions in the sequences with respect to other conserved blocks. For example, the annotated motif is ESTDEES. For Q6GR07, there are 2 possible motifs: GSTDEEQ and SKTGKEH, that appear close to the annotated motif in the alignment. We chose GSTDEEQ because it has a higher sequence identity with the annotated motif.
2. The motif defined in ELM is ..(T)..[DE], so in Q1DTN6, there are 2 overlapping occurrences of the motif: DETATES and TATESDR. We chose the second one to avoid introducing a new gap in the alignment that was not just by the sequence identity with the first motif.

BBS12034

Alignment BB12034 from reference set RV912

BAliBASE its sequence alignments on SAP structure alignments when possible, but there are some discrepancies between SAP and BAliBASE alignments. T-Coffee Expresso also uses SAP to produce structure based alignments, and their results also differ from the reference alignment. For example the 'DRTG' segment starts at position 33 in the Expresso and SAP alignments, while in the reference alignment it is shifted to the left.
Why was it decided that this segment should be shifted to the left, although it is aligned differently in the structure alignment?

Answer:

The SAP alignments were manually refined to align residues with known functions. In this case, in 1aiq, the motif DRTG contains a residue R in contact with a ligand (according to the pdb sum website). The same function is defined for 1b02 in the motif DVRTK.


While we agree that the two arginines in contact with a ligand should be lined up together it is still not clear to us why aren't they included in the core-blocks. We have knowledge about their importance and about them being equivalent, but it is not penalized if a method does not align them properly.
Another discrepancy between the SAP and BAliBASE alignments appears in the segment between positions 50 and 55 (in the SAP alignment). SAP decides that there is a deletion of the glutamine at position 52, but BAliBASE overrules the structure alignment and inserts the gap below a glycine, two residues to the right. Manual inspection of a WHATIF structure alignment leads us to a conclusion that, unless more knowledge is available about these residues, the SAP alignment is correct, and not BAliBASE. RV913_BOX158

This example shows that BAliBASE motif score might not always be the optimal measure for quality of motif alignments. BAliBASE scores this alignment using a KDEL motif that is described by the regular expression: "[KRHQSAP][DENQT]EL$". Here we present 3 alignment excerpts, with motif scores 0.37, 0.88, and 0.78, respectively. Each alignment is shown in two versions - once with BAliBASE motifs highlighted (orange) and once with KDEL pattern matches highlighted (purple). In alignment 1, which gets the worst score, the actual KDEL motifs are aligned perfectly. The best scoring one - alignment 2 aligns one residue incorrectly. In alignment 3, which also gets quite a good score, the motif residues are aligned with each other, but because of our poor alignment software gap parameter settings the motif is so spread out that it is hardly possible for a bioscientist to draw relevant conclusions. KMAD was explicitly designed to avoid such problems thereby optimally helping the bioscientist to analyze alignments.
There is no KDEL (or KDEL equivalent) motif present in the sequences other than at the very end. Thus, the KDEL motifs that are not at the end of the sequence should be backed up with some (experimental) evidence to be treated as the arbitrary truth for benchmarking purposes.
Thus, the 'FN' motifs should not be used as an 'arbitrary truth' for the benchmarking purposes.
In the 11th sequence BAliBASE annotated the motif as a 3-residue segment ("DEL"), but we suppose it was not done on purpose.

RV913_BOX142

In this alignment the highlighted motif is a C-mannosylation site (ELM ID: MOD_CMANNOS). The regular expression ("W..W") doesn't match the 4th and 5th sequences. Moreover, the C-mannosylation site is a motif for attachment of a mannosyl residue to a tryptophan (the first of the two tryptophans in the 'W..W' motif), and in fourth and fifth sequence this first tryptophan is not present.
Again, there probably are good reasons to overrule the ELM motif, but we believe that automatic methods haven't progressed to the state that they should get (penalty) points for making or not making a similar decision.

RV913_BOX146

The motif is a clathrin box (ELM ID: LIG_Clathr_ClatBox_1). Its regular expression is "[IVLMF].[IVLMF][DE]" The motif pattern matches the fourth sequence in a different location than annotated by BAliBASE - much further from the C-terminus. Obviously one would not like to use this outlying motif for the motif score, but the penalty points given for an alignment of a motif that is not there might negatively influence the optimization of motif alignment software. The BAliBASE alignment is probably correct, but the alignment of this segment should, in our opinion, be included only in the general score and not in the motif score.

RV913_BOX082

The motif in this example is an SH2 ligand (ELM ID: LIG_SH2_STAT5, regex: "(Y)[VLTFIC].."). The pattern does not match the segment in the last sequence.
This alignment illustrates a problem with the BAliBASE motif benchmark. If there are several instances of a motif in an alignment BAliBASE only scores for one instance. Thus an alignment that would get two of the motif columns aligned right but the rightmost column wrong, would get a lower score than an alignment where only the last column is well aligned.

RV913_BOX132

The motif in this alignment is a PIKK phosphorylation site (ELM ID: MOD_PIKK_1), with regular expression "...([ST])Q..".
