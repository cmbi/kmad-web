Why did we develop KMAD?
The reason for developing KMAD was our opinion that sometimes, in case of disordered (and less often, but structured as well) proteins plain amino acid sequence is not enough to create a biologically relevant sequence alignment. Here is a very simple example to visualize the problem that we want deal with (red residues are phosphoserines):




The first alignment is what all methods that don't take into account any other information than amino acid sequence would produce. In terms of the amino acid of the amino acid similarity it is the optimal solution. However, we should remember that the best alignment is the one that represents the biology the best, and not the one that is the best solution to a mathematical equation. Hence, the alignment wher two phosphoserines are aligned together is in this case a better alignment - despite the fact that it has more gaps and pairiwise identity is lower.
What can it help you with?
KMAD alignments can be used for all the usual applications of alignments, but it can especially help in determining the conservation of PTMs and motifs in very divergent (but not only) regions. Therefore, it can also be helpful in supporting PTM predictions. This example shows how bad alignment can impair our conclusions about PTM conservation and reliability of prediction. (This is of course a very short alignment, just to illustrate the general idea, normally we wouldn't draw any conclusions from such a short excerpt). The red residues are phosphoserines and orange indicates a phosphorylation prediction. The second alignment shows that the phosphorylation is highly conserved in this region, which is not clearly visible in the first alignment. The second alignment also shows that the phosphorylation prediction is actually much more reliable than what it seems from the first alignment.
